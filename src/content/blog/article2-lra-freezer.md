## 💡 The Beginner's Breakdown: What LRA Actually Means

Let's keep this simple.

Your chest freezer has a compressor inside it — a mechanical motor that pumps refrigerant through the walls to keep everything frozen. That compressor does not run constantly. It cycles on and off roughly every 30 to 45 minutes depending on the temperature and how full your freezer is.

Every single time that compressor tries to start, it is fighting against pressurized refrigerant gas from a complete dead stop. Starting a heavy motor from zero requires a massive, split-second jolt of electricity — far more than the motor needs once it is already spinning.

Electricians call this the **Locked Rotor Amps** — LRA. It is the current the motor draws in the fraction of a second before it gets moving.

Here is the problem in plain numbers:

- My chest freezer **runs** at 172 watts
- My chest freezer **starts** at 954 watts

That gap — between 172 watts running and 954 watts starting — is where cheap batteries fail. A battery rated for 500 watts continuous output looks perfectly fine on paper for a 172-watt freezer. But the moment that compressor tries to start at 954 watts, the battery's internal safety circuit trips and the whole unit shuts off.

Your freezer goes dark. Your food starts warming. And you do not find out until it is too late.

## 🛠️ How to Find Your LRA in 2 Minutes

This is the most important thing you will do today. It takes two minutes and it will tell you exactly what backup battery your freezer actually needs.

**Step 1:** Pull your chest freezer away from the wall. You need to see the back panel.

**Step 2:** Look for a silver or white sticker — sometimes called a data plate or rating plate. It is usually on the back of the unit near the compressor, or sometimes on the inside rim of the lid.

**Step 3:** Find the number labeled **LRA**. It will look something like: LRA: 8.3 or Locked Rotor Amps: 11.2

**Step 4:** Multiply that number by 120 (standard US household voltage).

```
LRA × 120 = Your surge watts
```

**Examples:**
- LRA 5.0 → 600 surge watts
- LRA 7.5 → 900 surge watts
- LRA 8.3 → 996 surge watts
- LRA 12.5 → 1,500 surge watts

**Step 5:** Add 20% safety margin. The battery you buy needs a surge rating at least 20% above your calculated surge watts.

If your surge watts are 996, you need a battery with at least 1,200W surge capacity. Not 1,000W. Not 999W. At least 1,200W with room to spare.

## Why Most Battery Reviews Never Mention This

Here is something that bothers me about the way backup power gets marketed.

Every battery company leads with the big capacity number — 1,000Wh, 2,000Wh, 3,600Wh. That number tells you how long the battery will run something. It says nothing about whether the battery can start a compressor motor in the first place.

A battery with 1,000Wh of capacity and only 500W surge output will fail on your chest freezer every single time — even though it has plenty of stored energy. The energy is there. The delivery speed is not.

The three numbers that actually matter for chest freezer backup power are:

**1. Surge capacity** — Can it start the compressor?
**2. Continuous output** — Can it keep running once started?
**3. Battery capacity in Wh** — How long will it last?

Most reviews talk about number 3. Almost none talk about number 1. Number 1 is the one that decides whether your food survives.

## ⚠️ The Most Common Mistakes

**Mistake 1: Trusting the Energy Guide sticker**
The yellow Energy Guide sticker on the front of your freezer shows annual energy usage in kilowatt-hours. It has nothing to do with surge watts. Ignore it for this calculation.

**Mistake 2: Buying based on running watts only**
"My freezer only uses 150 watts, so a 300-watt battery should be fine." No. The running watts are irrelevant if the battery cannot handle the startup surge.

**Mistake 3: Assuming newer is better**
Modern inverter compressors often have lower LRA than older models. But some budget freezers still use older compressor technology with high surge requirements. Always check the actual data plate regardless of the age or brand.

**Mistake 4: Only calculating the freezer**
If you plan to run other appliances during a blackout — a refrigerator, a well pump, a window AC — each one has its own LRA. The battery needs to handle the highest individual surge, not the combined total. Use the [calculator](/local-quote/) to check your full setup.
